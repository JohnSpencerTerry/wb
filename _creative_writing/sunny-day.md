The radio was on low. The kids were at the counter in their pajamas, eating Cheerios. She rinsed her bowl.

The segment was about the Iowa contract. Three more years, the announcer said. The Farmers Union vote had come in close but it had come in. The rates were holding. She heard about a quarter of it and put the bowl in the rack.

The piece moved on to history. The early pilot program in Dubai. The Phoenix proposal. The engineers had first envisioned it for cities running out of water, the announcer said, cities that would be uninhabitable inside a decade. The first paying customer had been a film crew in Morocco who wanted a clear sky for a week. She didn't know if that was true.

The younger one wanted more cereal. She refilled the bowl. The older one was reading the back of the box. She kissed them both on the top of the head and told her husband she'd be back by five.

The work bag was by the door where she'd put it the night before. She zipped her coat to her chin. The cold caught her face.

The car was cold for the first few miles. She let the heater catch up to itself. The road out toward the East End was mostly empty on a Saturday morning in February. A plow had been through earlier and the salt was still showing in long pale lines along the shoulder.

She turned down the long driveway and the snow stopped at the hedge in a clean line. She'd seen it before, not often. The grass on the other side was green. She rolled the window down an inch before she reached the gate. The air that came in was warm.

She parked in her usual spot near the side door and got out. Her coat felt wrong almost immediately. She left it open and went inside.

Inside, she did what she did every Saturday. Thermostat, water, the small loop through the rooms. She'd stocked the fridge Thursday but she opened it anyway and moved a few things to the front. The bubble was running. She could hear it through the screens, a low even sound. After a few minutes she stopped hearing it.

The text came in while she was rinsing the coffee pot. She finished the rinse and dried her hands before she looked.

*Change of plans, not coming out this weekend. Sorry for the late notice. Don't worry about anything else, see you next time.*

She read it again. She put the phone face down on the counter and finished what she'd been doing, the pot in the rack and the dishcloth on the hook. Through the kitchen window the lawn looked the same as it had ten minutes ago.

She picked the phone back up and opened her husband's thread. She typed something, deleted it, typed it again. *Bring the kids out after lunch.* His reply took a minute. *Ok.*

She kept working. The pool cover came off, wound onto its drum at the side of the deck. She wiped down the chairs and the small table. She laid out towels on the chaise.

She opened the fridge and stood in front of it for a moment. She'd stocked it Thursday — grapes, cherries, a small jar of olives, the cheese plate. She put the grapes and the cherries on a tray. The cheese she left. The bottle of seltzer in the door she took out and then put back. That one would keep.

She heard the car before she saw it, the tires changing pitch on the gravel. She went to the door.

The kids were out before it had fully stopped. The older one had a swim bag over one shoulder. The younger one was carrying shoes. They took three steps from the car and shed their coats where they stood. The older one looked up at the sky, then back at her, and smiled.

Her husband waved through the windshield and tapped the wheel and was already backing up. She waved back and watched the car go back down the drive, through the line, and into the snow.

She went down to the edge of the lawn near the hedge. Grass on one side, snow on the other, a cardinal on a branch beyond the hedge. She stood there for a while.

The older one came up next to her and looked at the snow. He didn't say what he'd come over to say.

The pool's warmer than the air, he said.

It is, she said.

The younger one was yelling for them to come back in. The older one turned and went, picking his way across the grass.

She sat on the deck with a glass of water. The sun was lower now but still warm. The bubble was holding.

The older one was floating on his back near the deep end. The younger one was diving for something at the bottom over and over. She didn't ask what.

She watched them have it.
